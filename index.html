Fujairah Under Fire: The Strategic Logic of the Attack

20 May 2026·6 min read

On 15 May 2026, Iran-aligned militias launched a coordinated attack on Fujairah, the UAE's primary oil export facility on the eastern coast of the Persian Gulf. The attack targeted storage facilities and export terminals, temporarily disrupting approximately 500,000 barrels per day of crude exports and 250,000 barrels per day of refined product flows — a combined 6.3% of global daily crude oil supply.

Why Fujairah Mattered

For context: the Strait of Hormuz, the world's most critical chokepoint, had been effectively closed for commercial traffic since early 2024. Iran maintained a de facto blockade enforced through repeated missile strikes on commercial shipping and the constant threat of escalation against any vessel attempting transit.

As long as Hormuz remained blocked, Fujairah became the escape valve. The UAE's ADCOP pipeline — a 1.5 million barrel-per-day connection running south and east from Abu Dhabi to Fujairah — allowed Gulf crude to bypass the strait entirely. Ships could load at Fujairah and sail directly to Asian markets without transiting Iranian-controlled waters.

That's exactly why it became the most strategically important point of the entire global energy crisis.

What Transited Through Fujairah

  • 7.4 million cubic meters of marine fuel sold in 2025 — fourth-largest bunkering hub worldwide after Singapore, Rotterdam and Zhoushan
  • 1.7 million barrels per day in crude and refined products — approximately 1.7% of global demand
  • ADCOP pipeline at 1.5 million barrels/day, bypassing Hormuz entirely, carrying Abu Dhabi's Murban crude directly to Asian markets

With Hormuz largely closed, Fujairah had become the primary relief valve for Gulf energy exports. That's exactly why it's a target.

The Strategic Logic of the Attack

Iran isn't attacking Fujairah to destroy it. It's sending a precise message: no escape route exists outside of our conditions.

As long as Fujairah worked, the UAE could export oil bypassing Hormuz. Asian refineries could receive Murban crude without depending on the Iranian corridor. The blockade was not total. After the attack, even that valve is at risk.

Supply Chain Consequences

The combined capacity of alternative pipelines — UAE's ADCOP, Saudi Arabia's Yanbu route, Iraq's Kirkuk-Ceyhan toward the Mediterranean — is approximately 9 million barrels per day. Hormuz before the conflict moved 20 million. The gap is unbridgeable.

Three Carriers in the Gulf: The Logic of the Continuous Cycle

28 Apr 2026·4 min read

Deploying USS Gerald R. Ford, USS Abraham Lincoln, and USS George H.W. Bush simultaneously is not a technical measure. It's a high-profile political signal — and those who can read the numbers understand exactly what it means.

The Firepower Numbers

Each of these vessels carries a Carrier Air Wing of approximately 65-70 aircraft: F/A-18 Super Hornets, F-35Cs, and electronic warfare systems. With a combined fleet of roughly 195-210 aircraft concentrated in a single quadrant, the United States holds offensive capability exceeding that of many national air forces.

The Logic of the Continuous Cycle

Having three carriers operating jointly isn't just about increasing firepower — it's about maintaining an uninterrupted 24/7 operational tempo.

While one carrier's flight deck is running at full capacity, the crew of another rests and technicians perform critical maintenance. The result is constant, relentless pressure that leaves no breathing room for adversary defences. No single carrier can do this. Three can.

The Logistics Cost Nobody Calculates

Three carriers in a single quadrant means an enormous logistical chain: fuel, munitions, spare parts, food, personnel rotation. A force projection of this scale costs approximately $3-4 million per day in direct operational expenses alone — not counting the impact on maritime insurance markets and Gulf commercial shipping routes.

Helium-3 and Rare Earths: The Moon as the Next Resource Frontier

24 Apr 2026·6 min read

NASA talks about exploration. But behind the Artemis missions, something much larger is taking shape: the moon could become the largest mine humanity has ever discovered.

The Rare Earth Problem

Rare earths are 17 chemical elements essential for producing batteries, semiconductors, electric motors, wind turbines, and smartphones. Today, 60%+ of global production is controlled by China — a monopoly that seriously concerns both the US and Europe.

The lunar surface is rich in them. Studies of rocks returned by Apollo missions estimate significant concentrations of neodymium, europium, lanthanum and other critical elements.

Helium-3: The Fuel of the Future

Helium-3 is an isotope extremely rare on Earth — we have approximately 15 kg of global reserves. On the Moon, bombarded by solar wind for billions of years, estimates suggest the presence of around one million tonnes.

A single tonne of Helium-3 could theoretically satisfy the annual energy needs of the United States.

Helium-3 is the ideal fuel for nuclear fusion — the technology promising near-limitless clean energy without radioactive waste. China, Russia and the US know this. It is no coincidence that all three powers have active and parallel lunar programs.

The Supply Chain Question

Extracting resources on the moon is not just a technological problem — it's a logistical problem of unprecedented scale. How do you transport materials across 384,000 km in an economically sustainable way? Who manages extraction rights in a territory without a state?

The Artemis Accords — signed by 50 countries including Italy — represent the first attempt to answer the last question. But the first two remain the most complex engineering and logistics challenges ever faced.

The space race of the 20th century was ideological. This one is economic. And this time, it won't end with a flag planted in the ground.
